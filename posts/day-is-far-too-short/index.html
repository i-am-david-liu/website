Day Is Far Too Short

Been keeping this to myself, but I've always felt that I operated on a 27-hour cycle rather than the standard 24. Seriously, I could use the extra three. It'll fix my sleep schedule, and I won't go to bed thinking about all the things I wanted to do but didn't have the time for.

I'm hesitant in sharing this because I know plenty of people who view the opposite, usually those with depression or general apathy. When I was first introduced to spoon theory, it made me completely reevaluate the processes behind motivation. I learned that motivation was something I took for granted. And to take it further, I learned that not everyone was driven to finish tasks as efficiently as possible; a good number of people struggle maintaining their executive functions, which can sometimes lead to self-neglect.

I sympathize with this. But, I find I have no basis in explaining or discussing these mental states, as I've never performed deep research into the topic or experienced it myself. To put it more succintly, I lack the credentials.

And I certainly feel terrible taking a U-turn and talking about the oppositeâ€”in that I expect myself to do too much within a certain amount of time, and get irritated when I fail to satisfy those impossible conditions.

I relieve this dissonance by reminding myself this is a space for my thoughts. I tend the garden and plant the seeds.

Nonetheless: the Day Is Far Too Short.

I like doing things. I get impatient about it sometimes, which prompts me to remind myself of the long run. Time, for me, is simultaneously an ally and an obstacle. It's an obstacle in that I might need to spend years working towards a single goal (conducting research in AI safety), yet it's an ally in that I have all the time in the world to do so. And, given time, I can learn to appreciate the value of patience.

I recently acquired Superintelligence by Nick Bostrom, and as I was flipping through it, I started thinking about how I would approach the material. Do I want to skim through it first? Should I annotate? How many pages can I read per day before I hit information overload? (Related: Reading books vs. engaging with them).

Then I started wondering about how fast I can read it. Should I start skipping paragraphs when they don't interest me? Should I skip boring chapters completely? It's a 324 page book; can I trust myself to finish it within two weeks while retaining a decent amount of crystallized knowledge?

This is a dangerous road to wander down. At some point, I started acknowledging the signs and considered an oppositional thought: what is the purpose in reading quickly?

I guess one of my consistent faults is a nagging impatience to achieve whatever I want as quickly as possible, never breaking to smell the flowers. I'll take that into account for the future.
