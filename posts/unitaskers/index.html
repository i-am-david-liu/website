Unitaskers

Unitaskers are things made for one specific purpose that fail at everything else. This is a popular term used in cooking, particularly when labelling strange utensils buried in the depths of your kitchen cabinet.

Examples of kitchen unitaskers include, in my opinion: egg timers, grapefruit knives, garlic presses, whatever the hell this is, and my personal favorite, egg boilers.

Not all unitaskers are useless. For example, pizza cutters are objectively the best tools for slicing pies, while salad spinners can rinse off leafy greens faster than strainers.

It also depends on how often you'll be using them. If you need to pit 200 cherries per day, maybe invest in a cherry pitter. Otherwise, you probably don't need one.

Q: Can't I just buy a cherry pitter and keep it in my cupboard? What is there to lose?

A: Sure. You can populate your kitchen with anything you wish. But unitaskers are just a segue to my argument: we ought to prioritize tools that can do many things, rather than tools that specialize.

This statement might sound obvious to you. A tool that can perform seven tasks effectively is generally better than a tool that can perform one task very well (assuming the tasks are drawn from the same pool). You want to find a minimum covering for your most common functions. The uncommon ones, like shucking oysters or pasta-rolling, can be unitasked.

This leads to a question: for some category of tasks, what is the minimum set of tools that maximize performance?

We don't have to focus on kitchens. What is the minimally viable set of tools for woodworking? For strength training? For setting up a home server?

Can expertise be represented as the "scaling up" of a set of tools, such that they maximally cover a category of tasks? (Compare the amateur painter who has two brushes large and small, and the artisan who has a brush for every stroke.)
