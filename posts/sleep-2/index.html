Sleep 2

01-22-23 511 words

On average, I sleep from 1:30 AM to 9:30 AM. It's skewed, but consistent; I think forming a consistent sleep habit is step one. Choosing to align your schedule with social norms / circadian rhythms is (an optional?) step two, yet I've heard strong arguments from the pro- and anti- contingencies, strong enough that I can't decide which to picket for.

I sleep late and wake late (compared to the population), but I don't have trouble re-adjusting my schedule when needed. Think of it as a super mild version of delayed sleep phase disorder, although I don't like considering it as a disorder at all.

Back in the summer of 2022, I slept from midnight to 8:00 AM, averaging. The transition wasn't chronic, but I always felt this nagging feeling behind my brain that said something didn't feel right.

On weekends during this period, I'd switch back to a 2:00 AM - 10:00 AM schedule. It's become my equilibrium state. I gravitate towards it.

Common knowledge says younger people tend to sleep later than their older counterparts. My expectations for the future regard my current sleep patterns as a phase; once I enter my 40's or something adjacent, I might end up becoming an early riser. Who knows? Beliefs change rapidly when you're open to them.

A few reasons why I think I sleep this way:

  • I have no wake-time obligations (I recently graduated and I'm catching my breath to live a little). Because of this, I naturally slip into what my body and brain prefers.
  • I strongly lean towards night life over "day life". The air feels cleaner; crisper. Fuzzy golden lamps bathe the streets and restaurants like little suns. Everyone's relaxed. I'm relaxed. And I love the sound of crickets and owls.
  • I feel most creative at night, so whatever weird art project I've taken on ends up spilling into the wee hours of the morn. I have trouble letting go of unsatisfied tasks; I hate leaving things half-finished.

My off-kilter sleep schedule reminds me of the push to delay school start times in the U.S. As predicted, many high-schoolers back this idea, while older teachers, especially those with young children, brush it off.

Waking up during high school was brutal; I would not make future generations live through such a demand. Early rising doesn't imply an early bedtime, either. I had to wake up at 6:00 AM, but I didn't feel tired until close to midnight. That's less than seven hours of sleep for a 16-year old, and I was unable to budge from that schedule for four years.

Honestly, my mind felt clearer and healthier when I got to college and had more opportunities to choose when I wanted to sleep. College also afforded a "to each their own" type of freedom for me and my peers; some of my friends preferred waking up at 8 or 9, while others slept until noon.
