Breaking your flow

This is a tough skill to learn.

Say you're on the third hour of debugging (or writing, painting, composing); you've hit that sweet spot where the rest of the universe has been pared away, and the only things left are your brain and the task at hand.

What you've entered is a flow state: an immersive, hypnotic experience where minutes fly by like seconds. It's the workaholic's nirvana.

I think flow is one of cognition's natural wonders, and we should all experience it at least once per week, whether deliberately or inadvertently. But a small catch to flow is we may sometimes get so wrapped up in whatever we're doing that we neglect everything else outside of the bubble. Just yesterday I postponed dinner by three hours because I was writing my daily blog post.

I believe that was a mistake. Writing a blog post shouldn't take priority over feeding myself at a decent time, no matter how many ideas per minute (i/m) I'm generating. It's one of those traits glorified in media, shoehorned into archetypes like the clumsy genius or misunderstood artist. I think we ought to be more careful with those displays.

It's hard to shift contexts during a flow state for reasons aforementioned. But life is unpredictable, and sometimes little tasks spring up that demand more attention. Like eating dinner, for example.

And only when there's a clear and present danger to our flow (e.g. your house is on fire) are we most often able to reconnect with reality. Yet a dual model of flow, existing as the Real World and the Flow World, is unoptimal, because the flow-reality converter has a 3,000,000 ms latency and isn't even guaranteed to work unless one of the Worlds become uninhabitable.

In other words, once you're in, you're in.

But surely, at some level of self-control and discipline, we ought to be able to wrangle flow into an accessible convenience rather than a bound contract. So here's an idea: the next time we're in a flow state, we should think to ourselves, "We're in a flow state." If you're a lucid dreamer, this strategy might sound familiar.

By pitching awareness to our flow, we can learn to seamlessly interface it with reality. No more skipping dinners or hearing your roommates wake up before you fall asleep. Flow becomes a tool; a user action. Toggle on, toggle off.

Of course, it's not as simple as that. And I don't know if the ideal version is even possible. (Maybe there's a mystic analogue tucked deep within the Himalayas?)

And here's another counter: if we're able to control our flow, wouldn't it cease to become flow in the first place? This curious mental state prides itself on a lack of cognitive control; it's attention on autopilot, essentially. Ultimately, "controllable flow" is something I have yet to experiment with. My hope is that awareness of flow won't act as a memetic hazard and destroy said mental state completely.
