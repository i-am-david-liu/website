Distracted learning

I was digging deep into AI safety prerequisites today, and I stumbled upon this forum post.

The OP introduced a method called just-in-time learning, which advocated solving problems / misunderstandings when they arise, rather than spending weeks or months prepping a solid foundation. The post also noted that doing (e.g. working on a project) was already highly coupled with learning, so why not jump into a project faster than you can handle?

I find this advice useful, mainly because it attacked a belief I never stopped to evaluate. Just-in-time learning runs counter to my present habit of consuming things meticulously and with great care, and I realized that although my one-thing-at-a-time procedure worked great at taming content, it's not so great at learning. I had conflated content, the funny colors on your TV, with knowledge, the stuff that rumbles around your brain. And I felt I needed to fix that, or at least acknowledge I made a mistake.

Over the past few weeks, I've accumulated a decent backlog of things to read. Notice how I say to rather than want to; through some unconscious mechanism, I had relegated learning to a procession of tasksâ€”the same experience I sought to disentangle from college. I was shocked (and slightly ashamed) when I realized how coldly I approached learning. Where was the fun? Where was the intrigue? The most recent "cool thing" I learned was that neural networks can be represented as a sequence of affine transformations (watch 2:00-8:00 if you're interested), and I can't immediately think of any examples which came before it.

Most of things I wanted to read were longform blog posts, but I'd say about 20% of them were papers. Some of the papers are 57 pages long, while others sit at an accessible 12. My initial plan, which I now have the right to label "stupid", was to pick one at random, digest it, take notes, and move on to the next.

In the forum post I referred to earlier, OP advised that reading papers sequentially wasn't the best strategy. Instead, they proposed "[reading] with the intention of getting distracted". The reasoning behind this is to allow you to find what is most interesting to you in that moment, as you'll care more about whatever you're reading rather than approaching it as a thing to conquer.

I'm interested in putting this into practice. I think it makes more sense to follow the path of steepest descent rather than mapping out the territory yard by yard. But I also have to be careful not to fall into sensation-seeking patterns, causing me to hop from one paper to the next in search of more satisfying ideas.

Another point OP made was not to take notes while reading papers; instead, one ought to write down questions, misunderstandings, and disagreements about the text. I agree with the idea behind writing down questions, but I'm not fully convinced note-taking is an anathema (they compare notes to exam prepwork, which only acknowledges one possible function of note-taking). I find that notes help me summarize the big ideas, and I don't have to trudge through 20 pages of 10-point font just to find the idea I'm looking for.

Overall, I'm grateful for that post. It gave me a reason to stop and reflect on what I'm doing. The earlier I can experiment with different methods, the faster I can cherry pick the ones that work the best; after that, I expect most of it to be fine-tuning.
